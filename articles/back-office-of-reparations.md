On 28 May, Ukraine's parliament [ratified](https://www.kyivpost.com/post/77014) a European loan worth up to €90 billion, 298 deputies in favour. The fine print says Kyiv only has to pay the money back once Russia pays reparations for the destruction it has caused. Three days earlier, in Luxembourg, Russia's central bank had quietly [filed a lawsuit](https://eutoday.net/russian-central-bank-opens-second-eu-court-challenge-over-frozen-assets-plan/) at the EU's own court, its second, against the rule that ties those reparations to Russia's frozen money. And two weeks before that, a court in Moscow had [ordered Euroclear](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/may/15/moscow-court-orders-euroclear-pay-compensation-russias-central-bank/), the Belgian company where most of that money sits, to compensate the Russian state for freezing it.

Put the three together and a strange shape appears. Russia's money is everywhere in this story. It shapes the loan, it sets off the lawsuits, it sits at the centre of every European summit. It is everywhere except the one place it would actually help: in Ukraine's bank account, paying for the war Russia started.

Let me be honest about why this is worth your time, because at first it looks like plumbing. It is plumbing. But it is the plumbing through which Europe's biggest moral claim of the decade either flows or does not, and right now it does not. What Europe does with roughly €193 billion of Russian state money is really a test of one question: is "make the aggressor pay" a promise Europe will keep, or just a slogan it likes to repeat? That question is being answered not in a room full of presidents or foreign ministers but inside an anonymous bank account, and almost nobody is watching the account.

## A workaround that looks like prudence

Start with the most generous reading, because it is a serious one.

Helping yourself to another country's national savings is not a small step. In modern times it has essentially never been done to a country the size of Russia. There is a rule against it, sovereign immunity, which simply means one government does not seize another government's property. That rule is not a Kremlin trick. It protects European money parked abroad exactly as much as it protects Russia's. Break it carelessly and you give every government in the world, and every nervous official in Riyadh or Beijing, a reason to wonder whether the euro is still a safe place to keep a nation's savings. A lot of Europe's financial weight rests on a boring promise: that it will not raid the vault for political reasons, however good the reason looks at the time.

So you can read the European choice as caution. Help Ukraine now, with real money, this year. Avoid setting a reckless example by grabbing the assets outright. Keep Russia formally on the hook, so the moral debt stays on the books. On this reading, the €90 billion loan is the grown-up compromise. Kyiv gets cash, the law stays intact, and Moscow's bill comes due later.

It is a coherent story. It is also, when you look closely, a list of the things Europe has decided not to do.

## Europe found a third option

We tend to assume there are two things you can do with an enemy's money: leave it alone, or take it. Europe found a third option, and the third option quietly became the policy.

The money is not handed back to Moscow. It is also not spent. It is *frozen in place*, parked in a permanent in-between, visible enough to punish Moscow and reassure Kyiv, untouched enough to spare Europe the example that spending it would set. In December, the EU used an emergency clause in its treaties, [Article 122](https://www.belganewsagency.eu/eu-member-states-agree-to-indefinite-immobilisation-of-frozen-russian-assets), to lock the assets down for good, by majority vote, so that no single country could ever hand them back to Russia. That took real nerve and it was also the easy half of the job. Freezing the money forever is politically cheap; doing something with it is where Europe stops.

This is the idea I would ask you to hold onto. Europe has not failed to imagine reparations. It has built a machine that talks about reparations while putting off the actual payment. The frozen billions have become a kind of security deposit on a promise, rather than a payment on a debt. The gap between those two things is the gap between a policy and a pose.

You can see it in the numbers. The plan Europe walked away from, the so-called reparations loan, would have put around [€140 billion](https://www.npr.org/2025/12/19/g-s1-102967/eu-leaders-agree-on-ukraine-loan) of the frozen cash to work, with Kyiv repaying only if and when Moscow paid up. What replaced it was a €90 billion loan that 23 of the 27 member states went out and *borrowed on the markets* instead. Europe chose to take on new debt rather than touch the pile it already controls. And it did so while happily handing Ukraine something else from that same pile: the interest. Frozen money still earns interest, the way any large balance does, and through a separate G7 [scheme](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy2744) Europe has been passing that interest straight to Kyiv. So the earnings on Russia's money are fair game; the money itself is sacred. The implication here is clear: it is not a law that draws that line because law doesn’t treat interests differently. Therefore, the line is a political one.

## The sanction lives in a bank account

To see why the line falls exactly there, look at where the money physically sits.

Of the roughly €210 billion in Russian central bank assets the EU has frozen, the [great majority, about €193 billion](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/euroclear-and-geopolitics-immobilised-russian-assets), sits on the books of a single company in Brussels called Euroclear. Most people have never heard of it, and that is the point. Euroclear is part of the machinery behind the financial system: when banks and governments buy and sell bonds, Euroclear is the place that records who owns what and moves the cash from one side to the other. Russia, like everyone else, kept money flowing through it. When the Russian bonds held there came due and turned back into cash, that cash had nowhere to go. It piled up, frozen, in Euroclear's accounts. The richest sanction in European history is, before it is anything else, an enormous balance sitting in one Belgian firm.

That is why the whole decision lands on one country. Belgium's prime minister, Bart De Wever, [refused](https://www.npr.org/2025/12/03/g-s1-100225/belgium-rejects-plan-frozen-russian-assets) to spend the money without "ironclad guarantees" that Belgium would not be left holding the bill. His foreign minister, Maxime Prévot, called the reparations loan "the worst of all" the options. Their fear is concrete, and it is already coming true: if Europe uses the money, Russia sues, and Russia is suing. The Moscow ruling against Euroclear in May is a taste of the retaliation to come, the kind that could, by Belgium's own estimate, leave it owing billions and leave Euroclear fighting court battles around the world. A decision cheered in 27 capitals would be paid for by one.

So the real obstacle is practical more than it is legal. Acting means somebody has to carry the risk, and as things stand that somebody is whichever country happens to host the plumbing. Belgium did not volunteer to become the keeper of Europe's most dangerous liability. It simply is, and it is behaving the way any government would when handed a continent-sized risk to carry alone.

## A payment problem, the wrong way round

When people reach for a reparations comparison, they reach for Versailles, usually to warn that crushing a defeated enemy with debt breeds the next war. That is not the lesson we should draw from this. There is one, narrower and more practical, and it came from John Maynard Keynes in 1919, in *The Economic Consequences of the Peace*.

Keynes spotted a problem nearly everyone else had skipped over. Deciding that Germany *owed* the money was the easy part. The hard part was actually moving it. To pay, Germany had to sell enough goods abroad to earn foreign cash, then ship that cash to the winners, year after year, without wrecking its own economy in the process. Owing the money and being able to deliver it turned out to be two completely different things. Economists still call this the [transfer problem](https://cep.lse.ac.uk/pubs/download/dp1155.pdf). It is why the 1920s turned into a financial merry-go-round: America lent money to Germany, Germany paid the winners, the winners repaid America and the same dollars went round in a circle until 1931, when the music stopped and the whole structure fell over.

Now look at 2026 and notice that the problem has flipped to the other side. In 1919 the difficulty sat with the loser: could Germany find the money? In 2026 the money is already here. It is already inside the European system, already counted, already frozen, sitting in euros in Brussels. Russia does not need to earn or ship anything. Its ability to pay was never the issue, because in a sense it has already paid, into an account Europe holds. The only thing left to settle is whether Europe is willing to move money it is already sitting on.

That is a genuinely new situation: Keynes's Germany could not pay; Europe's Russia, in a sense, already has, into an account Europe already holds. The money is there, the political will is what’s missing.

## A risk that could be managed

The honest version of this argument has to admit the risk is real. Belgium is not hiding behind paperwork. Euroclear is a load-bearing wall of the European financial system, and asking one country to expose it to endless Russian lawsuits, seized assets abroad and the slow loss of its reputation as a safe, neutral place to do business is no small thing. Belgium's caution is a reasonable answer to a danger that landed on it by pure accident of geography.

But a real risk can still double as a convenient excuse, and here it does both. Because the danger is genuine, nobody has to admit it could be defused. But here is the key point: this risk could be defused. The thing that makes Belgium's exposure unbearable is precisely that it is Belgium's, and Belgium's alone. A risk that would crush one mid-sized country is perfectly survivable when 27 of them carry it together.

So we arrive, again, at the root of the problem: how Europe shares risk. Defusing this one would mean doing the single thing Europe has spent a decade dodging, which is taking on a serious liability in common.

## Everyone is covered except the claimant

Let me be plain about who this arrangement protects and who pays for it, because that is the part the press releases leave out.

The Commission is covered: It gets to announce that Europe is funding Ukraine, on time, in the tens of billions. The cautious member states are covered: They keep the principle that national savings cannot be seized, which protects their own money too. Belgium is covered, or at least less exposed, having forced everyone else to admit the liability it was being handed. Euroclear is covered: Its books stay clean. Even Russia is partly covered: It keeps legal ownership of the money, a running grievance and a courtroom weapon it is already swinging.

The one party not covered is Ukraine: The country with by far the strongest claim on this money gets, instead of a payment, a loan, and a loan that comes with strings attached, including a [wartime tax overhaul](https://www.army-technology.com/news/ukraine-eu-defence-loan/) it had to pass before the funds were released. The aggressor's frozen fortune is treated as untouchable. The victim's future tax revenue is treated as something to borrow against. Behind the loan stands the EU budget, which means European taxpayers, quietly borrowing on the markets to do what the frozen account could have done directly. The money that belongs, in every moral sense, to the people Russia bombed is the one pot everyone has agreed not to open.

This is the political heart of the matter and it is worth saying without softening. Europe has arranged things so that the cost of defending Ukraine falls on Ukrainians and on European taxpayers, while the assets of the country that caused the destruction sit untouched, earning nothing but the right to sue. "Russia will pay" is repeated so confidently precisely so that Russia does not have to, at least not yet.

## What it would take to spend it

None of this is a call for recklessness, and the alternative is not "grab the money and hope." The alternative is the dull, doable work that Europe keeps pointing at and refusing to start. It comes in three parts, and none of them is beyond what the EU can already do.

The first is to share the risk. Belgium balks because the lawsuits would land on its books, so move them off. A joint EU guarantee, signed by every member state, promising to cover any bill Russia's lawsuits produce, turns an unbearable national risk into a manageable shared one. Europe has built exactly this kind of common safety net before, for the pandemic recovery fund and for the very loans it is already raising for Kyiv. The tool exists. What is missing is the decision to use it here.

The second is to wall off the plumbing. The mechanics that the [Centre for European Reform](https://www.cer.eu/insights/ukraine-reparations-loan-how-fix-europes-financial-plumbing) and others have laid out are not exotic. Move the frozen cash into a separate company set up for this one job, and have that company, not Euroclear, make the loan to Ukraine. Then if Russia sues, it sues a structure built to absorb the blow, instead of one of the firms that keep the financial system running. This is reparations engineered so the transfer does not break the system around it, the exact thing Keynes said the 1920s never managed.

The third is to use the law Europe already used. The EU froze these assets for good under Article 122, by majority vote, over Hungarian and other objections. The same majority that made the freeze permanent could make the spending real. The legal design of the reparations loan, where Kyiv repays only once Moscow pays and the assets are used rather than outright seized, was built precisely to stay on the right side of the immunity rule. It can be argued in court. Russia will sue either way; it is already suing over the freeze itself. There is no safe option here. Europe is choosing between two risks, and it has picked the one that costs Ukraine the most.

These are not radical ideas. They are the bare minimum of a reparations policy that wants to be more than a press release, and every one of them sits inside powers the EU already holds. They do not need a new treaty. They need the willingness to carry a shared liability, which is the same willingness Europe keeps postponing on defence, on borrowing, on enlargement, on every file where acting means somebody, somewhere, accepting a cost today for a benefit that is shared and delayed.

## The verdict

The test of Europe's seriousness was never whether it could freeze Russian money. It has shown that it can: €210 billion locked down for good by a vote no single country can undo. The test is whether it can turn that frozen balance into an actual settlement, and so far Europe has built something colder than a refusal. It has built a system whose finest achievement is keeping the question open.

So the money stays where it is, in the back office, neither returned nor spent, frozen carefully enough to spare Europe the example of using it and invoked loudly enough to let Europe claim it is making Russia pay. Frozen \*for\* Ukraine, in the official language. Frozen \*from\* Ukraine, in the only ledger that counts, the one in Kyiv.

A continent that can find €90 billion on the markets but cannot bring itself to spend the €193 billion already in its hands has not run out of money. It has run out of the nerve to decide whose money it is. That decision is still sitting in an account in Brussels, waiting for someone in Europe brave enough to make it.
