On 24 April, Reuters obtained an internal Pentagon email proposing that Spain be suspended from NATO. Madrid's offence was that, alongside Rome and Paris, had refused to let US aircraft use Spanish bases (Naval Station Rota and Morón) to strike Iran. The email also suggested Washington should reconsider its support for British rule of the Falklands, as a warning to an obstructive London. Asked about the threat the next morning, Pedro Sánchez replied with aplomb: *"We do not work off emails. We work off official documents and government positions."*

Perhaps the line of the season. Not only because the NATO Treaty has no mechanism for suspending a member, only for voluntary withdrawal, but because of how the line was delivered. It has been since 2003 that a European prime minister hasn't spoken to Washington in this register. Sánchez, Meloni and Macron, leaders who converge on little else, had flatly said no to the war. Keir Starmer, after a fortnight of dithering, agreed only to *"defensive missions"*. Even Pope Leo XIV, on his Africa tour, told reporters he had "no fear of the Trump administration" and called for the US and Iran to return to talks. Trump replied with an AI-generated picture of himself as Christ, which he later deleted.

It is tempting to read all this as the moment when Europe found its voice. There are some elements that support the reading: Spain shut its bases; Italy and France refused overflight; the President of the European Council António Costa told the press that *"the European Union is not part of the conflict"*. This is more than was said in 2003, when Schröder and Chirac protested but the Italian and Spanish governments of the day marched. It is more than was done in 1991, when even Mitterrand sent French troops to the Gulf. The capacity to refuse is real and it has grown.

But refusal is not policy. The European *no*, on close inspection, is also indicative of what Europe has *not* done. It has not contested the legality of the American blockade of Iranian ports, which stands on flimsy ground. It has not convened the oil buyers' club that the economists Isabella Weber and Gregor Semieniuk have been publicly proposing, an obvious move for a bloc that takes a quarter of the world's crude oil and could, with Japan, South Korea and Singapore, drive a bargain on price. It has not advanced any new diplomatic vehicle of its own. Through the Ramstein air base in Germany, it has continued to provide the logistical backbone of the war. As the German sociologist Wolfgang Streeck has noted, this gap between stated distance and practical support is now a familiar European pattern. **Europe has, in short, refused without proposing.**

## Hormuz as Suez?

There is a precedent for this kind of half-event. It is worth dwelling with some caution. In late October 1956, British, French and Israeli forces invaded Egypt. The pretext was Nasser's nationalisation of the Suez Canal Company; the real motive, on the British and French side, was to hold on to an imperial position the Americans had judged untenable. Eisenhower, who had not been consulted, reached for the only weapon that mattered: money. He instructed his Treasury to threaten a sale of American holdings of British government debt, which would have crashed the pound. He blocked Britain from receiving emergency loans from the IMF. The Bank of England lost $45 million in days. The British chancellor, Harold Macmillan, told Prime Minister Eden the country could not afford to import food and oil within weeks. The ceasefire came at midnight on 6 November. Eden's career was over by the new year. Five months later, the leaders of Germany, France, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands and Luxembourg were in Rome signing the treaty that became the European Economic Community.

It is not a small thing that the Treaty of Rome was signed five months after Suez. Negotiations had been going on for years, of course; the plans were not invented overnight. But the speed of the Six in those five months, as well as the willingness to bind themselves into a customs union, an agricultural policy and a court of justice, owed something to the lesson of Suez. The lesson was not that America was hostile but that depending entirely on the US was a structure with limits. The institution they built was the response to those limits. Britain, having drawn the opposite lesson, that the special relationship had to be repaired at any cost, sat the founding out and spent the next sixteen years trying to get in.

The 2026 mirror is obvious, almost too neat. An American administration launches a war without consulting its allies. The allies refuse the bases and the overflights. Washington reaches for the instruments of discipline, Spain's NATO seat, the Falklands, the trade deal with Britain. Yet, Europeans hold the line. The roles have been reversed: it is Europe now refusing the imperial adventure and Washington that finds itself out of leverage. A Suez moment then.

## What is different

But the analogy needs to be handled with care. Three differences matter.

**The first is the direction of refusal.** In 1956, the humiliated powers were Europeans retreating from a conquest of their own. In 2026, the overreaching power is American and the European role is to decline involvement, not to be forced out of it. That changes the meaning of the refusal. Europe in 1956 had to invent something to replace an empire it had lost. Europe in 2026 has to invent something to *confirm* a refusal it has already made. The first is more dramatic. The second may be harder to achieve.

**The second difference is leverage.** Eisenhower's, the dollar and the bond market, broke Eden in a week. Trump's instruments, tariff threats, withdrawn intelligence, the leaked Pentagon email, are blunter and slower. They operate in a world where (1) the dollar is no longer the only game in international trade, (2) the European Central Bank has spent fifteen years building reserves and (3) no European prime minister now wins votes by capitulating to Washington. Sánchez, governing on a knife edge, gains by saying no. Meloni, whose electorate has cooled on Atlanticism, gains by saying no. Even Starmer, who would prefer not to gain anything, gains a little.

**The third difference and the most important, is that there is no project.** In 1956, the leaders of the Six did not have to invent the European Economic Community out of nothing. The plans were already on their desks. The political imagination existed because, for ten years, a generation of Europeans had decided that integration was the answer to the German question, to French insecurity and to American patronage. In 2026, European leaders are coming up empty. What ideas there are come out of national leaders' dockets. There is the Weber and Semieniuk article. There is Costa's vague phrase about the EU being "part of the solution". There are commentariat slogans, strategic autonomy, geopolitical Europe, sovereignty, that have been making the rounds for a decade without anything binding to show for them.

Nevertheless, the analogy is worth keeping because Suez teaches that humiliation does not, by itself, produce new institutions or arrangements. The Six built the EEC because they had a plan and a political class trained to pick it up. The British, who were equally humiliated, did not. **We are now in the British position.** The materials for a different European foreign and economic policy exist, the economic historian Adam Tooze has laid them out, but the politicians who would translate them into institutions are missing. The LSE in late March called Europe a "strategic spectator". The spectator status is not from absence of opinion. It is from absence of vehicle.

## What can Europe do?

What would a vehicle look like? Weber and Semieniuk have given one answer. An oil buyers' club, with the EU at the centre and the major Asian importers as natural partners, could cap the price of physical-delivery crude oil at $100 a barrel: generous to producers, but a firm ceiling against the panic that has Sri Lanka now paying $286 a barrel and even European refiners paying $150 for North Sea oil. Low-income countries would be allowed to keep their pre-war import volumes; club members would absorb the missing 25 per cent through coordinated reductions. Refiners would have to give up windfall profits in exchange for guaranteed access. The instrument is technically straightforward, the authors compare it to the Allied raw-materials boards of the First World War and politically inflammatory in the way the moment requires. Convening it would be the first time in a generation that the EU practised a foreign economic policy distinct from Washington's.

It would not be enough on its own. The European Council would have to follow with a real energy emergency plan: not the petrol-tax cuts that have become the reflex in Rome and Paris (politically easy, economically clumsy and in the end harmful to the people they are supposed to help), but the kind of demand-rationing that the 1970s actually required and that Brussels has never had the courage to coordinate. It would have to commit, finally and in writing, to the electrification timetable that the battery price collapse and France's nuclear surplus already make possible. It would have to take seriously the argument that Europe cannot hold both the Iranian and the Ukrainian fronts forever. And it would have to call the war an aggression, in the language used by the hundred international-law professors whose open letter has so far been ignored, because the rules-based order Europe claims to defend cannot survive its own selective application.

These are not radical proposals. They are the minimum content of a European refusal that wishes to be more than a press release. They are also, every one of them, well within the existing legal and institutional capacity of the Union. They don't require new treaties, just old political imagination. The imagination that, in 1957, looked at the wreckage of an Anglo-French expedition and saw the outline of a customs union.

Europe in the spring of 2026 has done something it has not done in twenty years: it has said no and it has held the line. That this no has not become a project is not yet a verdict. But the months after Suez are the analogy that ought to keep European policymakers awake. The Treaty of Rome was signed in March because the politicians had been ready for it in November. There is no draft on the table this time. There is only the chance to start writing one and a war that, in narrowing every other future, has at least made the writing legible.
